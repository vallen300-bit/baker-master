The parties

  2. Substitute drywall subcontractor: Pichler GmbH (FN 66459 i, Landes- als Handelsgericht Graz; registered office Weizer Straße 13, 8162 Passail; Vienna branch Slamastraße 44, 1230 Wien; represented on the Werkvertrag by Hr. Malik and Fr. Kolb).

Background

  1. Brisen and Hagenauer signed an open-book, cost-plus contract.
  2. Brisen reimbursed Hagenauer monthly for the actual cost of all work performed, including amounts Hagenauer paid to its subcontractors.
  3. Hagenauer was trusted to manage those subcontractors and recover from any that failed.
  4. On subcontractor failure, Hagenauer had two duties: charge back to that subcontractor and credit Brisen, or absorb the cost itself if the failure was its own.
  5. Either way, Brisen was never supposed to carry the cost of a subcontractor's failure.

What happened with the drywall work

Hagenauer engaged RHTB as the drywall subcontractor. RHTB defaulted.

Hagenauer issued two formal cure notices — 7 July 2025 and 10 August 2025.

The second notice declared partial termination of RHTB's contract and announced that a substitute contractor would finish RHTB's scope at RHTB's cost.

Hagenauer brought in Pichler as that substitute, on a pure hourly basis (€68.80 per hour plus materials at supplier cost + 12%) — no fixed price, no original scope of work.

Pichler worked at the site from 16 July to 29 November 2025 and issued 19 partial invoices totalling €360,610 net.

On 26 August 2025 Hagenauer filed RHTB's defective acceptance protocol — which itself listed major drywall defects in 72 hotel rooms (all 36 first-floor rooms, all 36 third-floor rooms), wrong ventilation cut-outs in four lift lobbies, and missing inspection openings.

Despite this, Hagenauer paid each Pichler invoice in full and billed the cost through to Brisen in the monthly open-book account. Brisen reimbursed Hagenauer.

What Hagenauer failed to do

  • Did not issue a charge-back to RHTB for the substitute-contractor cost.
  • Did not credit Brisen the recovered amount in the open-book reconciliation.
  • Did not pursue RHTB for the shortfall or book it as a receivable.

Brisen's Arguments (to be validated)

  1. Duties crystallised payment-by-payment, before any termination.
    Each of the 19 Pichler invoices Hagenauer paid through the open-book account between 16 July and 29 November 2025 created a contemporaneous duty on Hagenauer either to charge that cost back to RHTB or to credit Brisen.
    Brisen was paying its bills on time throughout — no Brisen breach to suspend the duty.
  2. Termination releases future performance, not past duties.
    Hagenauer can argue forward-looking relief from any post-termination obligation.
    It cannot argue that its silent failure to charge RHTB through the second half of 2025 is excused because, months later, the contract ended.
  3. The claim is restitutionary, not performance-based.
    Brisen asks for return of money paid into the open-book account that should have been credited back.
    Recovery of overpayments survives termination of the underlying contract; both ordinary contract law and unjust enrichment support it.
  4. Hagenauer's likely defence fails on causation.
    Counsel may argue Brisen's later breach extinguished the credit duty.
    That defence requires a Brisen breach occurring before the recharge duty crystallised plus a causal link.
    Hagenauer never charged RHTB even in the months Brisen was paying on time — duty breached on the merits, not suspended.
  5. Independent legal opinion confirms recourse.
    Pochmarski Kober opinion of 4 December 2025 confirms Brisen's right of recourse against Hagenauer for failure to enforce subcontractor warranty.
    Date and firm to be reconfirmed by counsel before external use.
  6. Amount and evidence are anchored above.
    Every euro is tied to a paid Pichler invoice.
    Every step — open-book contract, cure notices, partial-termination declaration, RHTB acceptance protocol, 19 paid invoices, absence of any RHTB charge-back — is cross-checkable against Hagenauer's own records.
