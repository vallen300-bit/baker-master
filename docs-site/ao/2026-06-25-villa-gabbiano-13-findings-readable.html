Villa Il Gabbiano — Research Findings

Porto Cervo, Costa Smeralda · 25 June 2026
Deal timeline: signed 19 Jun · due diligence 3 Jul · preliminary contract 17 Jul · completion Sep 2026 · €22.5m + VAT · all cash · bought personally.
Bottom line. The villa is:
  • priced above the supportable range, in a market where almost nothing trades;
  • carrying permanent legal problems that pass to whoever owns it;
  • at risk of being frozen if the buyer is ever sanctioned;
  • and, on death with no will, locking into a shared ownership that can be forced to auction.
Two Russian-tax questions remain open for the buyer's own lawyers. The remaining checks are a proper valuation, and the buyer's Italian notary and Russian lawyer.
Price and how it compares
1Is €22.5m a fair price — and is there a real market to compare against?

Pulling together the little comparable data that exists, the villa is worth roughly €16–23 million, with a midpoint around €19 million.

  • The €22.5m + VAT asking price sits at the very top of that range — and probably about 15–20% above it.
  • But it is still inside what other villas are being asked for, so it cannot be called clearly absurd.
  • It is hard to be more precise because, at this price level, the market is almost invisible: hardly any completed sales are ever made public. Only a few headline trophy prices surface — Romazzino at €160m (March 2025) and Certosa at €350m.
  • Two reasons for the silence: very few villas trade at €15m+, and luxury sales are deliberately kept private under anti-money-laundering rules.1 The whole top end is roughly €1 billion of sales across about 150 deals — tiny and scattered.
  • The villa's own floor area, and how VAT applies to it, are not yet confirmed. The €19m midpoint is an estimate, not a proven figure.
Because there is essentially no visible market, there is no way to know whether the price is above or below what the next buyer would pay. A proper independent valuation, before the 17 July preliminary contract, is the only way to settle the price.
  1. Guardia di Finanza — Italy's financial police; AML = anti-money-laundering. The secrecy of this segment means roughly €1bn of sales across ~150 deals leaves almost no public price record.

Insurance
3Can a contested coastal villa be insured — and what about climate risk?
  • The insurance problem is about whether the building is legal, not about the weather.
  • If a building illegality1 has never been put right, the villa gets no catastrophe insurance and no State disaster aid.
  • If an amnesty2 is in progress it can be insured — but how long that "in progress" period lasts is undefined.
  • Ordinary voluntary insurance is then a question of price (or refusal) by insurers, not a legal ban.
  • Erosion of the granite headland is a minor, managed risk.
  1. abuso edilizio — a building or planning illegality (unpermitted works). If never regularised, it blocks catastrophe cover and State aid.
  2. sanatoria — an Italian building amnesty that regularises an abuso. While it is in corso (in progress) the duration is undefined.
The permanent legal problems
4How often are Sardinian demolition orders actually carried out (the ~23%)?
  • Confirmed: 23% in Sardinia — 602 of 2,622 orders were actually carried out.1
  • The correct national figure is 32.9%, not 15.3% (the lower number leaves Sardinia out).
  • Under Italian law,2 if a demolition order is ignored, the local council automatically takes both the building and the land, for free — so the owner can lose ownership without a single bulldozer turning up.
  • The order is permanent and stays attached to the property: whoever buys inherits both the illegal building and the order; selling it or waiting does not clear it; and it blocks both a resale and any mortgage.
Although about 75% of orders are never enforced, that is not reassuring: the order is permanent, passes to the buyer, blocks sale and finance, and can end with the council taking the property for nothing — a permanent cloud over the title, not an imminent bulldozer.
  1. Source for the enforcement rate: Legambiente / La Nuova Sardegna — 602 of 2,622 demolition orders carried out = 23% in Sardinia; 32.9% nationally (not the 15.3% figure, which covers five southern regions and excludes Sardinia).
  2. Article 31, Presidential Decree 380/2001 — if a demolition order is not complied with, the property passes automatically and free of charge to the municipality (acquisizione gratuita di diritto), taking both the works and the land. The order never times out and stays attached to the property — selling it or waiting does not clear it.
5The Gianluca Vacchi villa — where does the demolition / seizure saga stand?
  • A roughly €15m villa at Porto Cervo / Pantogia (Arzachena), entirely unpermitted,1 placed under criminal seizure2 in 2023 and confirmed by Italy's highest court.
  • It is still standing and still seized as of November 2025; a pre-trial hearing is set for 3 February 2026 — about four years of frozen, unusable ownership.
  • This is a criminal-seizure case — a different legal route from the council take-over described above.
It is a real-life example of years of limbo and frozen carrying costs. The case is still open until 3 February 2026, so its outcome is unknown.
  1. totalmente abusivo — entirely unpermitted construction.
  2. sequestro — criminal seizure of the property; here confirmed by the Corte di Cassazione (Italy's supreme court). This is a criminal route, separate from the Article 31 administrative process.
Sanctions, identity and inheritance
6Sanctions screen on the buyer — and could the villa be reached through Aelio or a family member?
  • The buyer is not on any sanctions list today.
  • But if he were ever listed, the villa could be frozen — because EU sanctions reach anything a listed person owns 50% or more of, or controls,1 and a March 2026 EU court ruling2 has tightened that test, making it easier to reach assets held through a holding company such as Aelio, or through a family member.
  • This has happened before: Deripaska, Mazepin, Usmanov, Mordashov and Aven all had Costa Smeralda property frozen in 2022.
  • The buyer is himself associated with Komos3, a large Russian agro-industrial group — a profile of exactly the kind that can attract a future EU designation.
The buyer is clean today, but a single future EU listing would lock up the villa, and the legal machinery to reach it through a holding structure already exists.
  1. The EU 50%-ownership-and-control rule — EU sanctions reach any asset owned 50% or more, or otherwise controlled, by a listed person, including through holding companies or family / nominee holders.
  2. EU Court of Justice case C-84/24 (judgment 12 March 2026, verified) — tightens the control test, strengthening the reach of a freeze through holding companies.
  3. Komos — a large Russian agro-industrial group based in Udmurtia, with which the buyer is associated.
7Named Russian-owned Italian trophy properties frozen since 2022?
  • Five named Costa Smeralda villas were frozen in 2022: Deripaska (Walkirie), Mazepin (Rocky Ram, ~€105m), Usmanov (~€17m, Arzachena), Mordashov (~€66m) and Aven (Punta Sardegna).
  • All are still frozen; none has been confiscated or sold.
  • The often-quoted "~€600m in Sardinia" total is unconfirmed (the national figure is roughly €2–2.6 billion).
  • Important distinction: a freeze locks the asset but does not take it away.1 The owner keeps the title, and the freeze depends on the EU listing — which can be overturned (as with Mazepin and Aven).
A Russian-owned villa here is visible, can be frozen, and can sit locked and unsellable for years — but the State does not seize it and sell it.
  1. A freeze is not a confiscation — it locks the asset but leaves ownership with the owner, and it falls away if the underlying EU listing is overturned (as happened with Mazepin and Aven).
8What happens on death, with no will, to one indivisible villa — spouse plus several adult children?

Bought personally with no will, the estate runs through a seven-step trap — all verified against the Italian Civil Code, updated 29 April 2026:1

  1. Split. The spouse gets one third; the children share the rest (about 2/9 each if there are three).2
  2. Joint ownership. The heirs own the whole villa together, with no one owning a specific slice3 — each holds an abstract share of the entire house, not a room or a wing.
  3. Every decision needs everyone. Selling, mortgaging or any major step needs the consent of all heirs4; any single heir can block it — and can equally block the others from blocking, so the house can freeze completely.
  4. The deadlock has no time limit. Any heir can demand the estate be divided, at any time, and that right never expires.5 It takes all of them to keep the peace, but only one to break it.
  5. Why it ends in a forced auction. The law guarantees each heir his value, not a physical piece. So the court works through a fixed cascade:6 (a) it first tries to give each heir a real part of the estate — but a single villa cannot be physically cut up; (b) it then tries to award the whole villa to one heir, who pays the others their shares in cash — but on a villa of this value no heir can fund the multi-million-euro buy-out; (c) with no way to divide it and no one able to buy the others out, the only remaining way to turn one indivisible house into shareable value is to sell it — so the court orders a public auction and splits the cash. Judicial auctions usually clear well below market: roughly 15–20% under appraisal at the start, then a further ~20–25% cut each time a round fails, compounding toward half value after several rounds.11
  6. Even if they all agree to keep it, the trap stays armed. "Everyone keeps the villa" is not a settled state — it is a truce that needs unanimous agreement, forever. Any heir can change his mind on any bad day (a divorce, a debt, a death that reshuffles shares, a family falling-out) and restart the forced-auction chain. Worse, a cash-strapped heir can sell his own share to an outsider12 — the others get a right of first refusal but must pay to use it, or end up co-owning the villa with a stranger.
  7. A will cannot fully fix it. Italian law reserves a fixed minimum share for close family7 that overrides any later "tidier" will. (The spouse's right to stay in the family home8 does not apply, because a holiday villa is not a family residence — so the surviving spouse is trapped alongside the children.)

Which country's law applies? EU rules9 point to Russian law, but Russian law hands immovable property back to the country where it sits,10 so Italian law governs the villa. The result is a split estate handled in two parallel proceedings.

Leaving the villa to a spouse and children does not create a clean inheritance — it creates a locked, multi-owner situation. Keeping it requires every heir to agree, forever; any one heir can, at any time, drag the house to a discounted forced auction or sell his share to an outsider, and a future will cannot fully prevent this. The tax cost is small; the perpetual deadlock is the problem.

  2. Articles 581 and 566, Civil Code — with no will, the spouse takes one third and the children share the remainder (about 2/9 each where there are three children).
  3. comunione ereditaria — the heirs' joint, undivided ownership of the inherited estate; no heir owns a specific physical part.
  4. Article 1108, paragraph 3 — selling the jointly-owned asset needs the consent of all co-owners (consenso di tutti); any single heir can therefore block a sale.
  5. Article 713 — co-heirs may always demand division (i coeredi possono sempre domandare la divisione); the right never times out.
  6. Articles 718 and 720 — the court must first try to give each heir a share in kind (Art. 718); where an asset cannot be conveniently divided (non comodamente divisibile) it is assigned whole to the heir willing and able to pay the others out, and only if no heir takes it on is there a sale by public auction (Art. 720: se nessuno dei coeredi è a ciò disposto, si fa luogo alla vendita all'incanto). The auction is mandatory, not discretionary, once the earlier steps fail.
  7. The reserved share (legittima), Articles 537 and 542 — a fixed minimum owed to close family that a later will cannot defeat.
  8. Article 540 — the surviving spouse's right to occupy the family residence; it does not attach to a holiday villa.
  9. EU Regulation 650/2012, Article 21 — succession is governed by the law of the country where the deceased habitually lived (here, Russia).
  10. The "hand-back" rule (renvoi), Article 34 → Russian Civil Code Article 1224 — for immovable property, Russian law applies the law of the place where the property sits, which sends the villa back to Italian law and produces a split estate. (The EU/Italian mechanics are well-settled; the Russian-law step should be confirmed by a short Russian-counsel opinion before any external use.)
  11. Article 591, Code of Civil Procedure — when a judicial-auction round draws no buyer, the court reduces the base price for the next round; successive failed rounds compound the discount, which is why forced sales routinely clear far below open-market value. (The number of months between rounds is a practitioner estimate, not a fixed figure.)
  12. Article 732 — if one co-heir sells his share, the other co-heirs have a right of first refusal (prelazione) at the same price; if they do not exercise and pay, the share can pass to an outside buyer who then co-owns the villa with the family.

  • No Russian lawyer has been engaged; that is for the buyer's own side to decide.
  • The buyer already moves money offshore for other commitments, so moving funds out is not, in itself, a blocker.
  • A non-binding, preliminary orientation1 suggests: if paid from offshore money, it is mostly reporting paperwork; if paid with money taken out of Russia, there is real currency-control friction; and Russian Government-Commission approval2 for buying from an "unfriendly-state" seller is probably not required, but this is genuinely uncertain.
Two questions remain genuinely open for the buyer's own lawyers: (1) where the money comes from (offshore versus fresh money out of Russia); (2) whether Government-Commission approval is needed because of the seller. These are unpriced risks on a €22.5m commitment that cannot be undone, and existing corporate transfers do not set a precedent for a personal purchase.
