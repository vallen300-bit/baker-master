▸ 📋 VAT Clawback — EUR 5.77M
New Austrian legislation. Retroactive. Funds redirected to tax office.
▸ EUR 4.43M — New Austrian law on luxury apartments

Legislation: Betrugsbekämpfungsgesetz 2025 (Anti-Fraud Act 2025) — new Austrian legislation.

Change: Luxury residential apartments with a sale price exceeding EUR 2M can no longer claim VAT refund on construction costs. Previously, these refunds were standard practice.

Impact: EUR 4.43M in apartment sale proceeds that were expected to flow into the project were instead directed to the Austrian tax office as VAT clawback.

Timing: This law was enacted after construction had already begun and after the financial model was established. It was impossible to anticipate or plan for this retroactive change.

▸ EUR 1.34M — VAT mismatch between entities

Issue: A EUR 1.34M VAT mismatch arose from invoicing between the project's different legal entities (HotelCo and ApartCo/BD). This is a structural VAT issue related to the multi-entity project setup.

Combined with the EUR 4.43M clawback, the total VAT impact on the project is EUR 5.77M — funds that were expected to be available but are not.

▸ 🔨 Construction Settlement Outstanding
No final close-out. EUR 5.6M unpaid. EUR 5M guarantee lost. EUR 7M claim filed.
▸ Open-book contract — no final close-out

Contract type: Open-book with maximum price guarantee. This means all costs are transparent, with a guaranteed ceiling.

Status: The final invoice was never received. Hagenauer became insolvent on March 27, 2026, before the construction accounts could be formally closed out. This means the final reconciliation between work completed, amounts paid, and amounts owed is still unresolved.

▸ EUR 5.6M — Completed works unpaid

Situation: Approximately EUR 5.6M worth of construction work was completed by Hagenauer and his subcontractors but remains unpaid.

Why unpaid: Under the open-book contract, payments were staged against verified work completion. The final tranches were due but Hagenauer filed for insolvency before they could be processed.

Consequence: Subcontractors who performed this work were not paid by Hagenauer, despite the fact that the project had allocated funds for it. This is why subcontractors now refuse to return for defect repairs — they are owed money by Hagenauer.

▸ EUR 5M — Bank guarantee lost

What it was: Hagenauer provided a EUR 5M bank guarantee (Bankgarantie) as security for the proper completion of the construction contract. This is standard practice in Austrian construction — it protects the client if the contractor fails to perform.

What happened: When Hagenauer filed for insolvency, the bank guarantee was drawn upon by the insolvency administrator to satisfy claims from other creditors. The guarantee that was meant to protect our project was consumed by the insolvency estate.

Impact: This EUR 5M was meant to be available as a safety net for exactly the situation we now face — contractor failure and incomplete works. Its loss means the project bears the full cost of completing and repairing the work without this backstop.

▸ EUR 7M claim filed — limited recovery expected

Claim filed: EUR 7M Forderungsanmeldung (claim registration) filed against the Hagenauer insolvency estate. This covers: defective works, uncompleted works, overpayments for work not delivered, and costs of engaging replacement contractors.

Expected recovery: In Austrian insolvency proceedings, unsecured creditors typically recover 5–20 cents on the euro. Given the size of Hagenauer's liabilities and the number of creditors, realistic recovery is expected to be minimal.

Timeline: Insolvency proceedings in Austria typically take 12–24 months. Any recovery will be slow and uncertain.

▸ EUR 4M settlement fund for insolvency proceedings

Purpose: Approximately EUR 4M must be reserved for legal costs, expert assessments, and settlement negotiations as part of the formal insolvency proceedings.

Why necessary: To protect our claims and position in the insolvency, we need specialized insolvency counsel, construction experts to quantify defects, and funds to participate in creditor committee proceedings. Without adequate legal representation, our EUR 7M claim risks being reduced or subordinated.

▸ 💥 Contractor Insolvency — March 27, 2026
Hagenauer filed insolvency. All subcontractors ceased. Double cost on every repair.
▸ What happened

Hagenauer GmbH, the main construction contractor for the RG7 project, filed for insolvency (Insolvenzantrag) on March 27, 2026. This occurred while construction works were still active and before the project could be formally closed out.

Hagenauer was responsible for the complete construction delivery including all trades — structural, mechanical, electrical, finishing, and specialist historic restoration works. Their insolvency immediately halted all subcontractor activity on the project.

▸ Financial impact — EUR 5.6M unpaid works + EUR 5M guarantee lost

EUR 5.6M completed works unpaid: Subcontractors completed work that was invoiced by Hagenauer but payments from Hagenauer to his subcontractors were not made. The project had allocated these funds, but they were consumed within Hagenauer's insolvency.

EUR 5M bank guarantee lost: The performance guarantee meant to protect the project was absorbed into the insolvency estate. This safety net no longer exists.

EUR 7M claim filed: Formal claim registered with the insolvency court. Expected recovery: 5–20 cents on the euro, over 12–24 months.

Net exposure: The project is effectively carrying the full cost of completing and repairing all outstanding works without recourse to the original contractor or his guarantee.

▸ Operational impact — subcontractors will not return

Every subcontractor who worked under Hagenauer is owed money. When contacted for defect repairs, the universal response is: "Hagenauer did not pay us. We are not coming back."

This means every outstanding defect — windows, electrical, KNX, plumbing, finishing — requires sourcing a new contractor at full market price. These new contractors demand upfront payment because they see a project where the main contractor went bankrupt.

Double cost effect: Work that was already paid for (through payments to Hagenauer) must now be paid for again to new contractors. Every defect costs twice.

▸ 📜 Austrian Tax Law Change — 2025
EUR 5.77M redirected to tax authorities. Law enacted after construction began.
▸ The law: Betrugsbekämpfungsgesetz 2025

Austria enacted the Betrugsbekämpfungsgesetz 2025 (Anti-Fraud Act 2025), which changed the VAT treatment of luxury residential property.

Previous rule: Developers could claim VAT refund on construction costs for all residential apartments, regardless of sale price.

New rule: Apartments with a sale price exceeding EUR 2M are excluded from VAT refund on construction costs. The VAT already refunded must be clawed back.

Timing: The law was enacted after our project's financial model was established and after construction was underway. There was no way to anticipate or structure around this change.

▸ EUR 5.77M total impact

EUR 4.43M — Direct VAT clawback on apartment sales. Sale proceeds that should have flowed into the project were directed to the Austrian tax office.

EUR 1.34M — Additional VAT mismatch between invoicing entities within the project structure.

Total: EUR 5.77M — These funds were included in the original financing plan as available project resources. Their redirection to the tax authority is the second-largest single contributor to the current funding gap.

▸ 🪟 Revenue Locked in Unhanded Apartments
Service charges unpaid. Rental income lost. Client relationships at risk.
▸ Daily cost of non-handover

Every apartment that cannot be handed over creates three concurrent costs:

1. Service charges borne by the project: Mandarin provides concierge, cleaning, and building management to all apartments. For unhanded units, the project pays these charges instead of the apartment owner.

2. Lost rental income: Handed-over apartments can enter the MO rental pool and generate revenue. Unhanded apartments sit empty — lost income every month.

3. Client relationship: Buyers who have paid for apartments and are waiting months for handover become increasingly dissatisfied. In the luxury segment, word travels fast. One unhappy buyer can influence multiple prospective purchasers.

▸ 📉 Asset Value at Exit
Sale process starting. Current performance determines price. Every month counts.
▸ Sale process timing and performance

The hotel sale process is being initiated with a leading international adviser. The hotel's current operating performance will be presented to potential buyers as part of the sale materials.

Better performance now = higher price. Hotel buyers and their advisers apply a capitalization rate to the hotel's trailing 12-month performance. Every month of improved performance (higher occupancy, higher ADR, lower losses) directly increases the achievable sale price.

Unresolved defects reduce value. Construction issues, unhanded apartments, and pending legal matters are all flagged during buyer due diligence and result in price adjustments or contingency holdbacks.

The investment made now to improve performance and resolve issues will be reflected directly in the exit valuation.

The cost of delay exceeds the cost of funding. Monthly operating losses, unpaid bank interest, and frozen apartment revenue compound over time. With the hotel sale process starting now, current performance directly determines the exit value.
