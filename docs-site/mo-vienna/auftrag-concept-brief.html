Frame · Section 2

The Reframe

What changed between v1 (this morning) and v2 (now), and why.

What v1 proposed

Use the empty shop as the world's first AI-run luxury residence showroom for the MO Residences Vienna, fronted by an AI agent named "Mira" or "Aria" qualifying buyers and walking them through floorplans on an interactive wall. Press hook: a Vienna analogue to Andon Market in San Francisco.

Why v1 was wrong

  1. The Vienna UHNW residence buyer pool is too small to justify a dedicated retail surface. A handful of qualified buyers per year cannot anchor a 160 sqm shop economics. The shop would be empty 95% of the time.
  2. AI is not a credible closer at €5–15M ticket. UHNW residence buyers do not buy from a screen. They buy from Anna Egger over a private dinner, or from a private broker. An AI showroom risks cheapening the very residences it is supposed to sell.
  3. The Andon Market precedent is a trap, not a tailwind. Andon is a Cow Hollow corner store selling granola, candles, and store-branded sweatshirts. NBC's headline: "AI runs this store. It's lied, surveilled workers and tried to hire someone in Afghanistan." That is the opposite of the press tone Mandarin Oriental will tolerate in its building.

What v2 proposes instead

The reframed brief — three jobs in priority order
  1. Job 1: The shop is a destination footfall driver into the MO Vienna bar. Every visitor — buyer or not — leaves having had a reason to walk into the hotel.
  2. Job 2: The shop generates tier-1 press as among the first AI-led retail concepts in European heritage luxury. It claims a category that is currently empty.
  3. Job 3: The shop sells real product at viable margins (€150–€3,000 ticket band).

Residence sales are not a job of this shop. They become a downstream byproduct of the Tier-3 private suite consultations (Section 7), not the purpose.

Frame · Section 3

The Concept — AUFTRAG

Working name: AUFTRAG (German for "the commission"). Backup: MAKER 1010.

One paragraph

Vienna invented the modern commission economy. Between 1903 and 1932, the Wiener Werkstätte under Josef Hoffmann, Adolf Loos, Koloman Moser, and Gustav Klimt operated a model where clients commissioned bespoke objects directly from masters. AUFTRAG revives that model with an AI as the designer-on-call. The visitor describes an occasion, a memory, or a person. The AI proposes designs — Hoffmann grids, Klimt florals, modernist restraint à la Loos — drawn live in ink by a pen plotter at the centre of the room. The visitor refines. The order is routed to the appropriate maker — Lobmeyr for crystal, Augarten for porcelain, Mühlbauer for hats, Knize for accessories, Backhausen for textiles. Some pieces are engraved live on-site. Others are produced by the maker's atelier in two to six weeks and shipped. Every piece carries an embedded NFC chip linked to a private digital lineage page.

What the visitor takes home

  • A one-of-one heritage object made by a Viennese master (the physical commission)
  • The original ink design proposal drawn by the plotter, signed and dated (the artefact of the conversation)
  • A digital passport linked to the piece: design lineage, maker's signature, firing/blowing/cutting timeline, personal note from the master
  • A complimentary visit to the MO Vienna bar (the host gift)

What the visitor does not get

  • An AI that talks to them in a chirpy voice
  • A glowing screen the size of a wall
  • An "AI assistant" with a name like Mira or Aria
  • The feeling of being in a tech showroom
"While Silicon Valley puts AI at the till, Vienna puts AI in the workshop."
— Working press line, to be tested with PRCO
Frame · Section 4

Why Now

Three timing arguments — press, white-space, first-mover — converge on a 12-18 month window.

1. The press window closes by late 2027

The "AI in physical luxury retail" story is currently uncrowded but will not stay that way. Cognizant's 2026 luxury retail outlook explicitly warns that the first-mover gap, once opened, will become too wide to close. Anthropic, Google, and Microsoft have all announced retail-AI tooling in Q1 2026. By late 2027 the novelty value of any AI-fronted retail concept will be heavily diluted. Our window is 12–18 months from now.

2. AI x physical luxury is genuinely empty white space

External research confirms: every named luxury-AI deployment to date is a clienteling tool, an e-commerce assistant, or a back-office system. Examples: LVMH + Google Cloud (advisor empowerment), Swarovski Digital Flagship (online), Ralph Lauren AI Stylist (online), Burberry AI clienteling (back-end). No major luxury house has put an AI agent at the centre of a physical store experience. Andon Market in San Francisco is the only physical AI-run shop in the world — and it sells granola.

3. AI-perfume is already saturated. Heritage craft is not.

An earlier candidate concept — bespoke AI perfume — was killed during research. The slot is full:

  • Jo Malone Scent Advisor (December 2025)
  • Margiela Scentsorium (April 2026)
  • Generation by Osmo (2025)
  • Estée Lauder Paris Fragrance Atelier (2025)

By contrast, no AI x Wiener Werkstätte concept exists anywhere. The five Vienna heritage makers are family-controlled, taking commissions today, and unencumbered by exclusivity deals. This is the only AI luxury concept Vienna can defensibly own.

Opportunity The window to claim "AI x European heritage craft" as a press category is open and uncontested. It will not be open in 18 months.

Build · Section 6

The AI Role — Backstage Doctrine

The single design rule that separates AUFTRAG from every gimmick AI shop already attempted.

The doctrine in one line

Front-stage humans. Backstage AI. No exceptions.

Cognizant's 2026 research is unambiguous: in luxury, AI succeeds when it empowers the human host invisibly, and fails when it replaces them visibly. We hold this as a hard rule. The AI never has a voice. The AI never has a face. The AI never has a name. The AI is never quoted in press, never named in marketing, never anthropomorphised.

How the AI shows up physically

The AI's only outputs are physical. There are no screens larger than a tablet anywhere visible in the customer-facing space.

  • The pen plotter. A precision ink plotter sits on a heavy oak table at the centre of the room. As the customer describes what they want, the plotter draws design proposals live in ink on rag paper. The AI generates the path; the plotter executes it. The drawings are signed, dated, and given to the customer at the end of the session — whether or not they commission a piece.
  • The engraver. A computer-controlled engraver sits at the Lobmeyr station. The AI generates the path. The engraver executes it on the customer's chosen blank. The customer watches.
  • The kiln-bound order. For Augarten, Mühlbauer, Backhausen, Knize: the AI generates a production specification, signed off by the customer and the on-rotation master, and transmitted to the maker's atelier for production.

Front-stage: the human master on rotation

Each weekday, one of the five makers contributes a senior craftsperson to AUFTRAG for the day. They are the visible host. They authenticate, they advise, they sign each commission. Indicative rotation:

  • Monday — Lobmeyr
  • Tuesday — Augarten
  • Wednesday — Mühlbauer
  • Thursday — Knize
  • Friday/Saturday — Backhausen, alternating

This is not theatre. The masters are running their own commission books inside our space. We are providing them new clients.

RED LINE. The AI is never given a name in any external-facing material. No "Mira," no "Aria," no "Sofia." No press release attributes any quote to "the AI." Press refers to AUFTRAG as the atelier, never her or it.
Build · Section 7

The Heirloom Passport

Every commission ships with an embedded NFC chip linked to a private digital lineage page. The third evolution.

The premise in one paragraph

Cognizant's 2026 luxury retail outlook flagged scannable digital product passports (NFC / QR / RFID) as a structural pattern for high-end goods this decade — driven by EU Digital Product Passport regulation, the resale-authentication market, and the brand-storytelling opportunity at scale. AUFTRAG applies it natively. Every piece commissioned through the atelier carries a discreet NFC chip embedded in the base, the hatband, the seam, or the foot of the object. A tap of an iPhone opens a private digital page that lives forever. The chip is invisible. The story is unlimited.

What the passport contains

Three layers of provenance, one per page section
  1. Origin — the design conversation summary, the AI's design rationale, the lineage motif (which Hoffmann grid, which Klimt floral, which Loos restraint principle), the original ink plotter drawing.
  2. Provenance — the maker's signature, the master craftsperson's name, atelier metadata, photographs from the firing/blowing/cutting/sewing process, dated and timestamped.
  3. Personal — a handwritten note from the master, a dedication if the piece is gifted, an unlock-code mechanism so the giver controls when the page opens to the recipient.

Why this is more than a feature

  • It is a press hook on its own. "The AI heirloom that knows its own story." A photograph of a hand tapping a phone against a Lobmeyr glass and watching its lineage unfold is shareable in a way no other AI-luxury concept has produced. This is the social-media moment.
  • It is authentication infrastructure. The NFC chip is signed cryptographically. Every passport is verifiable. This becomes the foundation for an eventual Tier-4 offering: AUFTRAG-authenticated resale of pieces commissioned through the atelier. Cognizant flags the global luxury resale market at $360B by 2030.
  • It is a perpetual brand asset. The piece passes through generations. Each new owner taps the chip. The maker's signature, the AUFTRAG provenance, the MO Vienna address — all live forever on the object. We are not selling a product; we are creating an heirloom that documents itself.
  • It is the channel for re-engagement. The passport URL is the canonical relationship surface — anniversary reminders, refresh-piece prompts, new-collection invitations. No email database is needed; the object itself is the contact point.

One physical space. Three concentric layers of access, each feeding the MO Vienna bar/restaurant/suites differently.

Why three tiers, not one

  • Tier 1 generates the foot traffic and the press photography. Volume drives the bar funnel and the social media reach.
  • Tier 2 generates the unit economics. Atelier-hours conversion at €1,500 average ticket × 4 sessions/day × 200 days = ~€1.2M annual.
  • Tier 3 reopens the residence conversation that v1 tried to force. UHNW commissioning a €12K Wiener Werkstätte set in their MO suite is a far better residence-sales touchpoint than an AI showroom would have been. The conversation about a residence becomes natural, not transactional.
Strategic note Tier 3 is where the v1 premise (residence sales) gets resurrected — but as a hospitality byproduct of a craft conversation, not the purpose of the shop. This is materially better positioning for the residences themselves.

The MO Bar Funnel

How AUFTRAG specifically routes walk-ins and clients into the Mandarin Oriental Vienna bar, restaurant, and suites.

Mechanics by tier

Tier 1 — Walk-in funnel

Every visitor — purchaser or not — leaves with a complimentary Wiener Melange voucher redeemable at the MO bar within 24 hours. Engraving wait time (15–30 min) is reframed as "settle in next door": the master walks the visitor to the MO bar, the engraving is delivered to the bar table when finished. The bar becomes the natural sequel to the shop visit.

Estimated conversion: 55–70% of walk-ins → MO bar within 48 hours. Source: baker-marketing modelling.

Tier 2 — Atelier Hours funnel

Atelier appointments include a complimentary lunch at the MO bar restaurant during the design-development phase (the AI is generating production specs while the visitor eats). Lunch is part of the commission price, not a discount. The MO restaurant becomes the workspace of the commission.

Estimated conversion: 100% of Tier-2 appointments → MO bar/restaurant.

Tier 3 — Private Suite funnel

The master craftsperson and the AI plotter come to the MO suite. Multi-day consultation, multi-piece commission. The MO suite booking is the access mechanism. The hotel becomes the venue of the commission, not the venue of the shop.

Estimated conversion: Tier-3 clients are by definition MO suite guests.

Decide · Section 10

Risks & Mitigations

Honest assessment. Where data is thin, this section says so explicitly.

Risk 1 — MOHG brand fit (HIGH)

Mandarin Oriental's brand is built on discreet human service and restrained interiors. Any AI-fronted concept in their building risks reading as a brand violation. The risk is most acute at the launch press moment, when our tone of voice is hardest to control.

Mitigation:

  • The backstage-AI doctrine (Section 6) is the primary mitigation. AI is invisible; the human master is the host.
  • Brand architecture: AUFTRAG is an independent house, "at Mandarin Oriental, Vienna" appears only as address, never on the wordmark.
  • Pre-publication review by MOHG for the first 12 months of press coverage.
  • Informal Anna Egger temperature check before any feasibility spend (Section 10).
Risk 2 — Maker partnership timing (CRITICAL)

Without Lobmeyr and Augarten signed as anchors by end of June 2026, AUFTRAG collapses. Family-run heritage makers move slowly. They will want to see Brisen's seriousness before agreeing to multi-year exclusivity.

Mitigation: Director-led conviction meetings with Andreas/Leonid Rath (Lobmeyr) and Erwin Stoiber (Augarten) in May 2026. Partnership terms drafted by Buchwalder. Maker IP and signatory rights protected explicitly.

Risk 3 — Austrian / EU regulatory regime (MEDIUM)

The EU AI Act provisions taking effect 2 August 2026 require explicit disclosure when customers interact with an AI system. Article 50 is directly relevant. Risk of being classified as "high-risk AI" is low (we are not in scope of the high-risk annex), but transparency obligations apply. GDPR applies to any visitor profiling.

Mitigation: Buchwalder review of disclosure architecture before launch. Visible "this commission is co-designed with an AI system" signage at the entry to the atelier. No covert AI use.

Risk 4 — AI novelty press decay (MEDIUM)

The "AI in luxury retail" press story has a half-life of ~18 months. By late 2027, the novelty is gone and we need a durable narrative.

Mitigation: Lock the heritage-craft revival angle as the durable narrative from the start. AI is the delivery mechanism, not the headline. The story we want in 2028 is "the shop that revived Vienna's commission economy" — not "the shop with the AI."

Risk 5 — Foot traffic shortfall on Riemergasse (LOW–MEDIUM)

Riemergasse is a calm cross-street, not a Stephansplatz-density walk-by. Tier 1 walk-in volume of 33/day is a working assumption, not a measured number.

Mitigation: Pre-launch concierge agreements with competing Vienna luxury hotels (Sacher, Bristol, Park Hyatt) to send their guests. PRCO press launch generates initial destination traffic. To be pressure-tested by baker-deal-analyst against actual Vienna luxury retail benchmarks.
