## 2. GERMANY -- PRIMARY TAX ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Grunderwerbsteuer (GrEStG) -- THE CENTRAL RISK

**This is the single largest tax exposure in the entire transaction.**

#### 2.1.1 Section 1 Abs. 3 GrEStG -- Share Deal Trigger

Under sec. 1 Abs. 3 GrEStG, Grunderwerbsteuer (real estate transfer tax, "RETT") is triggered when:
- At least 90% of the shares in a company owning German real estate are **unified in one hand** (Vereinigung), directly or indirectly; or
- At least 90% of the shares are **transferred** to a new shareholder.

**The 90% threshold was reduced from 95% effective 01.07.2021** (Gesetz zur Aenderung des Grunderwerbsteuergesetzes).

**Application to this case:** Brisen Ventures Ltd (CY) would acquire 100% of shares in Lilienmatt. This **unambiguously triggers sec. 1 Abs. 3 GrEStG**. There is no threshold question -- 100% consolidation in a single acquirer is the textbook triggering event.

#### 2.1.2 Rate

Baden-Wuerttemberg GrEStG rate: **5.0%**

(Baden-Wuerttemberg has maintained 5.0% since 2011. Other Bundeslaender range from 3.5% to 6.5%.)

#### 2.1.3 Tax Base

The GrEStG tax base under sec. 8 Abs. 2 GrEStG (for share deals triggering sec. 1 Abs. 3) is the **Grundbesitzwert** (property value) determined under the Bewertungsgesetz (BewG), not the purchase price of the shares.

For development projects, the Grundbesitzwert is typically determined by the Finanzamt using:
- For undeveloped land (unbebaute Grundstuecke): Bodenrichtwert x plot area
- For developed land: Ertragswertverfahren or Sachwertverfahren
- For projects under construction: value of land + construction costs to date

**CRITICAL UNKNOWN:** We do not have Lilienmatt's financial statements (2024 or 2025 draft), nor the current Grundbuch extract, nor a property valuation. The Grundbesitzwert must be estimated by KPMG.

**Indicative calculation (for illustration only):**

| Scenario | Estimated Grundbesitzwert | GrEStG at 5.0% |
|----------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Low (land value only, early stage) | EUR 5,000,000 | EUR 250,000 |
| Mid (land + partial construction) | EUR 10,000,000 | EUR 500,000 |
| High (near-completion valuation) | EUR 20,000,000 | EUR 1,000,000 |

**KPMG must obtain:** Current Bodenrichtwert for Annaberg plots, construction cost to date, and any existing Grundbesitzwert Bescheid from the Finanzamt.

#### 2.1.4 Who Pays?

Under sec. 13 GrEStG, the tax liability falls on **both parties** (Gesamtschuldner). In practice, this is typically allocated contractually. Given the restructuring is being done to benefit Brisen/Vallen structures, it would likely be borne by the acquirer (Brisen Ventures).

#### 2.1.5 Section 6a GrEStG -- Group Restructuring Exemption

Sec. 6a GrEStG provides a RETT exemption for intra-group restructurings (Umstrukturierungen im Konzern) under certain conditions:
- The transaction must be between an "herrschendes Unternehmen" (controlling entity) and a dependent company
- The controlling entity must hold at least 90% for 5 years before AND 5 years after the transaction (Vor- und Nachbehaltensfristen)

**Application:** This exemption is **NOT available** here because:
1. Brisen Ventures does not currently hold any shares in Lilienmatt (no pre-existing 90% holding)
2. The current shareholders (AO, Ilana, Brisen Capital SA) are not in a group relationship with Brisen Ventures
3. The 5-year pre-holding period cannot be satisfied

**Conclusion: No sec. 6a GrEStG relief. Full GrEStG applies.**

#### 2.1.6 Section 5/6 GrEStG -- Transfer Between Related Parties

Sec. 5 Abs. 2 and sec. 6 Abs. 3 GrEStG provide exemptions for transfers between Gesamthaender (partners in a partnership). These do NOT apply to GmbH share transfers.

Sec. 3 Nr. 4 GrEStG exempts acquisitions from spouses. This applies to direct real estate transfers, NOT to share deals under sec. 1 Abs. 3.

**Conclusion: No spousal or partnership relief available for this share deal structure.**

### 2.2 Capital Gains Tax -- Sellers' Exposure

#### 2.2.1 Andrey Oskolkov (67.5%)

**Tax residency:** Germany (Baden-Baden). As a German tax resident, AO is subject to German income tax on worldwide income including capital gains on the disposal of GmbH shares.

**Classification:** Sale of shares in a Kapitalgesellschaft by an individual.

**Tax treatment under sec. 17 EStG:** Since AO holds more than 1% of the Stammkapital, any gain on disposal is taxable under sec. 17 EStG (Veraeusserung von Anteilen an Kapitalgesellschaften).

- The gain is subject to the **Teileinkunftverfahren** (partial income method): 60% of the gain is taxable at AO's personal income tax rate
- Assuming a top marginal rate of ~45% + 5.5% Solidaritaetszuschlag = effective rate on 60% = approximately **28.5% effective rate** on the total gain
- Alternatively, if AO holds the shares as Privatvermoegen (private assets, which is the case here), the gain under sec. 17 EStG is subject to the Teileinkunftverfahren, NOT the flat Abgeltungsteuer of 25%

**Tax base:** Sale price minus acquisition cost (Anschaffungskosten). AO's original acquisition cost is likely the nominal value of EUR 16,875 (or whatever he paid at formation in 2012 / subsequent acquisition). If the sale price is nominal (EUR 16,875 for a EUR 16,875 Nennbetrag), there is no gain. But the German Finanzamt will scrutinize whether the transaction is at arm's length.

**CRITICAL ISSUE -- Transfer Pricing / Fremdvergleichsgrundsatz:**

If the shares are transferred at nominal value (EUR 16,875 for 67.5%) but the fair market value is significantly higher (because the GmbH owns real estate worth millions), the Finanzamt will:

1. **Impute a deemed sale at fair market value** (verdeckte Gewinnausschuettung or Schenkung depending on the relationship)
2. Assess capital gains tax on the difference between FMV and nominal value
3. Potentially treat the undervalue transfer as a **mixed gift/sale** (gemischte Schenkung)

If AO is selling to Brisen Ventures (controlled by DV, who is not a family member of AO), a below-market transfer is NOT a gift -- it is a commercial transaction, and the Finanzamt will apply **sec. 1 AStG** (Aussensteuergesetz) and general transfer pricing principles to impute arm's length value.

### 2.3 Treatment of Shareholder Loans

Baker's data indicates AO has made significant shareholder loans to Lilienmatt (regular capital transfers of EUR 80K-200K tranches observed in Jan-Feb 2025, plus loans from Lilienmatt to MRCI).

**Tax treatment of loan assignment:**

1. If AO assigns his shareholder loans to Brisen Ventures as part of the transaction:
   - The assignment price determines whether AO recognizes income or loss
   - If loans are assigned at face value: no gain/loss for AO
   - If loans are assigned at a discount (because the GmbH cannot fully repay): AO may recognize a loss, but deductibility is limited under sec. 17 Abs. 4 EStG (Aufloesungsverlust) or sec. 20 EStG
   - For the GmbH: if the loan is forgiven or assigned at a discount, the difference may constitute taxable income (Ertrag aus Forderungsverzicht) unless it falls under the "wertlose Forderung" exception per BFH case law

2. **Transfer pricing on loan terms:** If AO's loans carry below-market interest, the Finanzamt may impute arm's length interest (verdeckte Einlage). KPMG should review the loan agreements.

3. **Grunderwerbsteuer interaction:** Under the current regime, shareholder loans are generally NOT included in the GrEStG tax base for sec. 1 Abs. 3 transactions (the base is the Grundbesitzwert of the real estate, not the enterprise value). However, if the loan assignment is structured as part of the overall purchase price, this needs careful documentation.

- However, if the share transfer triggers any deemed income at the GmbH level (e.g., from loan forgiveness), this would be subject to Gewerbesteuer in Baden-Baden (Hebesatz: currently ~400%, effective combined rate with KSt ~30%)

### 2.5 Withholding Tax

- This applies regardless of the buyer's residence (Cyprus)
- Germany retains taxing rights over gains from shares in real-estate-rich companies under most DTTs (see sec. 49 Abs. 1 Nr. 2 lit. e EStG for non-residents, but AO is a German resident so this is moot)

## 3. CYPRUS -- ACQUIRER TAX ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Brisen Ventures Ltd as Acquirer

**Cyprus does not levy tax on the acquisition of shares in a foreign company.** There is no Cypriot equivalent of GrEStG.

### 3.2 Capital Gains Tax (CGT)

Cyprus CGT (20%) applies only to gains from disposal of:
- Immovable property situated in Cyprus
- Shares in companies holding immovable property in Cyprus

Since Lilienmatt holds German (not Cypriot) real estate, **Cyprus CGT does not apply** to Brisen Ventures on any future disposal of the Lilienmatt shares.

### 3.3 Corporate Income Tax on Dividends / Future Disposal

- Cyprus CIT rate: 12.5% (as of 2026)
- **Participation exemption:** Under sec. 8(23) of the Cyprus Income Tax Law, gains from disposal of shares in a subsidiary are exempt from CIT, provided the subsidiary does not own immovable property in Cyprus. Since Lilienmatt owns German real estate, this exemption **applies** -- the gain is from shares in a company holding non-Cyprus immovable property.
- **Dividend exemption:** Dividends received by Brisen Ventures from Lilienmatt would be exempt under sec. 8(22) of the Income Tax Law (no minimum holding period or percentage requirement for foreign dividends, provided the paying company is not more than 50% invested in activities generating non-arm's-length income). This should be straightforward.

### 3.4 EU Parent-Subsidiary Directive

- The EU PSD eliminates withholding tax on dividends from subsidiary to parent within the EU
- Germany -> Cyprus qualifies (both EU member states)
- Minimum holding: 10% for at least 12 months (will be satisfied immediately as Brisen Ventures will hold 100%)
- **BUT:** Germany does not levy withholding tax on dividends from a GmbH to a corporate shareholder in any case (the WHT is eliminated under domestic law for EU recipients meeting the PSD conditions)
- The PSD is therefore a **belt-and-suspenders** protection but not the primary mechanism

### 3.5 Substance Requirements

**This is a key risk area.** EU anti-avoidance rules and German anti-treaty-shopping provisions (sec. 50d Abs. 3 EStG) require that the Cyprus holding company has genuine economic substance.

Brisen Ventures must demonstrate:
1. **Physical office** in Cyprus (not just a registered agent)
2. **Qualified directors** resident in Cyprus who make real decisions
3. **Board meetings** held in Cyprus with documented minutes
4. **Bank accounts** in Cyprus with signatory authority
5. **Adequate staff** or outsourced functions proportionate to the holding
6. **Business rationale** beyond tax optimization

**Risk assessment:** If Brisen Ventures is a shell with no substance, the German Finanzamt could:
- Deny treaty benefits under sec. 50d Abs. 3 EStG
- Apply the German GAAR (sec. 42 AO) to recharacterize the structure
- Look through Brisen Ventures to the UBOs (DV and Edita in Switzerland)

**Recommendation:** KPMG should review Brisen Ventures' current substance profile and recommend enhancements if needed. Constantinos Pohanis (Brisen Group, Cyprus) would be the natural person to ensure substance is in place.

### 3.6 Transfer Pricing Documentation

Under Cyprus TP rules (effective since 2022, based on OECD guidelines):
- Brisen Ventures must maintain TP documentation for any controlled transactions with Lilienmatt (intercompany loans, management fees, etc.)
- If Brisen Ventures charges management fees to Lilienmatt, these must be at arm's length
- If Brisen Ventures provides financing to Lilienmatt (replacing AO's shareholder loans), the interest rate must be benchmarked

## 4. SWITZERLAND -- BRISEN CAPITAL SA (GENEVA)

### 4.1 Disposal of 7.5% Participation

Brisen Capital SA (CHE-465.537.957), Geneva, holds 7.5% (EUR 1,875 Nennbetrag) in Lilienmatt.

### 4.2 Capital Gains Treatment

Under Swiss federal and cantonal tax law:
- Corporate capital gains are **included in taxable profit** (there is no general corporate capital gains exemption in Switzerland for non-qualifying participations)
- **Beteiligungsabzug** (participation deduction) under Art. 69-70 DBG / Art. 28 StHG:
  - Applies to capital gains on the sale of participations of **at least 10%** OR with a fair market value of at least CHF 1 million
  - Brisen Capital SA holds only **7.5%** in Lilienmatt
  - The FMV threshold of CHF 1 million could potentially be met if the 7.5% stake is valued above CHF 1M, but given Stammkapital of EUR 25,000 and the fact that the GmbH likely has significant liabilities (shareholder loans from AO, construction financing), the FMV of 7.5% may or may not reach CHF 1M

**Scenario analysis:**

| If 7.5% FMV | Beteiligungsabzug? | Tax on Gain |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Below CHF 1M | NO -- full taxation | ~14.0% (Geneva effective CIT rate) |
| Above CHF 1M | YES -- participation deduction available | Effectively ~0% on the capital gain portion |

**Geneva cantonal rate:** Combined federal + cantonal + communal CIT in Geneva is approximately 14.0% (post-STAF reform).

### 4.3 If Gain Qualifies for Beteiligungsabzug

If the FMV exceeds CHF 1M, the participation deduction operates as a proportional reduction of tax. The gain is included in taxable profit but the total tax is reduced proportionally to the ratio of participation income to total income.

**Practical effect:** If this is Brisen Capital SA's only significant transaction, the Beteiligungsabzug could reduce the effective tax close to zero.

### 4.4 Withholding Tax

- There is **no Swiss withholding tax** on a Swiss company selling shares in a foreign company
- The gain is a matter of Swiss corporate income tax only

### 4.5 Intra-Group Consideration

If Brisen Capital SA and Brisen Ventures Ltd are both controlled by the Vallens, the transfer could be structured as an intra-group contribution (Einlage/apport) rather than a sale. Under Swiss tax law, intra-group restructurings can be tax-neutral under Art. 19 Abs. 1 lit. d DBG (Umstrukturierung), but this requires:
- Transfer at book value (Buchwertprinzip)
- Continuity of taxation in the receiving jurisdiction
- The receiving entity must be a Swiss-taxable entity OR the Swiss taxing right must be preserved

Since Brisen Ventures is in Cyprus, a tax-neutral transfer is **unlikely** unless Switzerland retains the right to tax the gain (which it would not, as the shares would leave Swiss tax jurisdiction).

## 5. STRUCTURAL ALTERNATIVES

### 5.1 Alternative A: Balazs's Proposal -- Change Ownership Only at Cyprus Level

**Concept:** Instead of transferring GmbH shares directly, interpose a Cyprus company between the current individual shareholders and the GmbH, then transfer shares in that Cyprus company.

**Structure:**
```
Current:    AO (67.5%) + Ilana (25%) + Brisen Capital (7.5%) --> Lilienmatt GmbH
Proposed:   AO + Ilana contribute shares to NewCo (Cyprus) --> NewCo holds 92.5% of Lilienmatt
            Then: AO + Ilana transfer their NewCo shares to Brisen Ventures
```

**Tax assessment:**

This does NOT solve the GrEStG problem:
- Step 1 (AO/Ilana contribute to NewCo): If NewCo acquires >= 90% of Lilienmatt, sec. 1 Abs. 3 GrEStG is triggered at Step 1
- Step 2 (Transfer NewCo shares to Brisen Ventures): This is a transfer of shares in a Cyprus company, not a German company, so sec. 1 Abs. 3 GrEStG would NOT apply to Step 2 alone
- **BUT:** The German Finanzamt would almost certainly apply the **GAAR (sec. 42 AO)** to collapse both steps into a single transaction if done in quick succession, triggering GrEStG on the entire restructuring

**More importantly:** Step 1 itself already triggers GrEStG. So interposing a Cyprus entity first does NOT avoid the tax -- it merely moves the triggering event to an earlier step.

**Partial variant -- keep shareholding below 90%:** If AO and Ilana each contributed their shares to SEPARATE Cyprus entities, and no single entity crossed 90%, you might avoid sec. 1 Abs. 3. But:
- Post-01.07.2021 rules have an expanded lookback (sec. 1 Abs. 3a GrEStG) that counts shares held by related parties
- AO and Ilana are spouses, so their holdings would be aggregated
- Brisen Ventures and any Vallen-controlled entity would be aggregated if connected to the same Konzern

**Verdict: Balazs's approach does NOT provide GrEStG savings. It may simplify future corporate governance but does not reduce tax.**

### 5.2 Alternative B: Contribution (Sacheinlage) Instead of Sale

**Concept:** AO and Ilana contribute their Lilienmatt shares as an in-kind contribution (Sacheinlage) to Brisen Ventures in exchange for shares in Brisen Ventures, rather than selling for cash.

**Tax assessment:**

- **GrEStG:** Still triggered. Sec. 1 Abs. 3 applies regardless of whether the transfer is by sale, contribution, gift, or other legal act. The triggering event is the unification of >= 90% of shares in one hand.
- **Capital gains tax for AO/Ilana:** Under sec. 17 Abs. 1 EStG, a Veraeusserung includes any "Uebertragung gegen Entgelt." A contribution in exchange for shares in Brisen Ventures is an exchange (Tausch), which is treated as a deemed sale at FMV (sec. 6 Abs. 6 EStG). So capital gains tax is triggered just as in a cash sale.
- **However:** If AO/Ilana receive shares in Brisen Ventures, this could be relevant for **deferral under sec. 21 UmwStG** (Umwandlungssteuergesetz -- cross-border contribution). Under sec. 21 UmwStG, a contribution of shares in a German company to an EU/EEA company can be at book value (Buchwertantrag) if:
  - The receiving entity is an EU/EEA company (Cyprus = EU -- yes)
  - Germany retains the right to tax the contributed shares (this is problematic -- see below)
  - Application must be filed

**Problem:** After the contribution, Germany would no longer have taxing rights over the Lilienmatt shares (they would be held by a Cyprus entity, and Cyprus would have the taxing right under the CY-DE DTA). This means the Buchwertantrag under sec. 21 UmwStG would likely be **denied**, and the contribution would be at FMV, triggering full capital gains tax.

**Verdict: Contribution does not save capital gains tax for AO/Ilana, and GrEStG is triggered regardless.**

### 5.3 Alternative C: Step-by-Step Transfer Over Time

**Concept:** Transfer shares in tranches over several years so that at no point does any single shareholder (or related group) cross the 90% threshold.

**Assessment:**
- Brisen Capital already holds 7.5%. If only Brisen Ventures acquires AO's 67.5%, Brisen Ventures would hold 67.5%. Together with the related Brisen Capital (7.5%), the Brisen group holds 75% -- still below 90%.
- Then Ilana's 25% could be transferred later.
- **BUT:** The 2021 reform introduced sec. 1 Abs. 3a GrEStG, which aggregates holdings of "nahestehende Personen" (related parties). AO and Ilana are spouses. If the Finanzamt determines that Brisen Ventures is acquiring shares in a coordinated transaction, even staggered transfers could be aggregated.
- **Additional risk:** Sec. 1 Abs. 3b GrEStG (added 2021) creates a 10-year lookback. Any transfers within a 10-year window can be combined to determine if the 90% threshold is crossed.

**Verdict: Technically possible to structure around the 90% threshold for a time, but the 2021 reforms were specifically designed to prevent this. HIGH audit risk. Not recommended for a transaction where Aukera needs a clean structure anyway.**

### 5.4 Alternative D: Asset Deal Instead of Share Deal

**Concept:** Instead of transferring shares in Lilienmatt, have Lilienmatt sell the real estate to a new entity, then wind down Lilienmatt.

**Assessment:**
- GrEStG would still apply to the asset sale (direct real estate transfer, sec. 1 Abs. 1 GrEStG), at 5.0% of the purchase price
- Additionally, Lilienmatt would owe KSt + GewSt on the gain from selling the real estate (~30% combined)
- Notary fees, Grundbuch re-registration, and new financing documentation required
- **This is MORE expensive than a share deal, not less.**

**Verdict: Not recommended.**

### 5.5 Recommended Approach

**A direct share deal (current plan) is the most tax-efficient option available.** The GrEStG exposure cannot be avoided for a 100% consolidation in a real-estate-holding GmbH. The key optimization levers are:

1. **Minimize the purchase price for AO's/Ilana's shares** -- if the GmbH's liabilities (shareholder loans, construction financing) exceed or approximate its asset value, the equity value of the shares could be low or negative, minimizing capital gains tax
2. **Proper valuation** of the Grundbesitzwert -- engage a Bewertungsgutachter to support a conservative valuation for GrEStG purposes (the Finanzamt's initial Grundbesitzwert Bescheid can be challenged)
3. **Structure shareholder loan assignment carefully** -- face-value assignment avoids immediate income recognition for AO and the GmbH
4. **Ensure Brisen Ventures has adequate substance** before the transaction closes

## 6. EU ANTI-AVOIDANCE AND ATAD CONCERNS

### 6.1 ATAD I and II

The EU Anti-Tax Avoidance Directives (ATAD I, 2016/1164, and ATAD II, 2017/952) have been implemented in Germany, Cyprus, and (indirectly relevant) Switzerland. Key provisions:

| ATAD Rule | Risk Assessment |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Interest limitation** (sec. 4h EStG / sec. 8a KStG) | If Brisen Ventures provides loans to Lilienmatt, interest deductibility is limited to 30% of EBITDA (Zinsschranke). For a development company with limited operating income, this could restrict interest deductions. |
| **CFC rules** (sec. 7-14 AStG) | If Brisen Ventures is a low-taxed foreign company (Cyprus 12.5% vs Germany ~30%), German CFC rules could apply to attribute Brisen Ventures' income to German-resident shareholders. BUT: DV and Edita are Swiss residents, not German residents, so German CFC rules do NOT apply to them. AO, if he remains connected to Brisen Ventures, would be subject to German CFC rules -- but post-restructuring, AO should have no interest in Brisen Ventures. |
| **Exit taxation** (sec. 6 AStG) | If AO emigrates from Germany within 7 years of the share transfer, this is not directly relevant to this transaction but is worth noting for AO's personal planning. |
| **GAAR** (sec. 42 AO) | The general anti-avoidance rule is the primary risk. The Finanzamt could challenge the restructuring as having no business purpose beyond tax avoidance. **STRONG DEFENSE:** The restructuring has a clear business purpose (Aukera financing requirement), which is well-documented in correspondence. The Aukera term sheet and compliance requirements provide genuine commercial rationale. |

### 6.2 Principal Purpose Test (PPT) under MLI
