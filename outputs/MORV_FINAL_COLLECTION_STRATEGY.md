## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Sixteen of twenty-five residences at Mandarin Oriental Residences Vienna have been sold. Nine remain. This is not a problem. It is a strategic opportunity — but only if we reframe it correctly and immediately.

The danger is simple: sophisticated UHNW buyers do arithmetic. If the website says "25 private residences" and only 9 are available, the buyer concludes 16 were cherry-picked and what remains is what nobody wanted. This perception, once formed, is nearly impossible to reverse. It poisons every subsequent conversation, every viewing, every price negotiation.

The solution is equally simple: stop selling remaining inventory and start launching a final collection.

**The core principle: Never count down. Never say "9 remaining." Reposition as "9 released."**

One-line positioning: *"The first 16 buyers believed in the vision. The final 9 buyers get to live in the proof."*

This document provides the complete strategy across language, web presence, sales tactics, pricing, collateral, and marketing channels. It is designed to be executed immediately by Denis and Linassi+Co, with oversight from Dimitry. The total recommended investment is EUR 145,000-261,000 — representing 0.7-1.4% of the remaining inventory value, well within the industry norm of 1-3% for luxury residential marketing.

Three things can be done this week at zero cost that will materially improve the positioning. They are listed in Part 8.

## PART 1: LANGUAGE — WHAT TO SAY AND WHAT TO NEVER SAY

Language is the first line of defence. Every word on the website, in brochures, in email correspondence, and spoken by the sales team either reinforces the "curated collection" frame or confirms the "leftovers" fear. There is no neutral ground.

The following table must be treated as a binding style guide for all external communications — web copy, brochures, email templates, verbal scripts, and social media. Print it. Pin it above every desk.

| Never Say | Why It Is Dangerous | Say Instead |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| "Only 9 remaining" | Implies depletion. The buyer's mind completes the sentence: "...because nobody wanted them." Triggers loss aversion in the wrong direction — they fear buying what others rejected. | "A final collection of 9 residences" |
| "Still available" | The word "still" carries the entire weight of failure. It implies duration, waiting, disappointment. No luxury brand uses "still." | "Now released" or "Now accepting private viewings" |
| "25 residences" (anywhere public) | Invites arithmetic. The buyer reads "25," sees 9 available, calculates 36% unsold, and concludes either the product is flawed or the market has rejected the pricing. Neither perception is recoverable. | "A private collection of residences" — no total number, ever. |
| "Register Your Interest" | Pre-launch language. It signals the developer needs the buyer more than the buyer needs the residence. It is passive. It asks the buyer to wait. UHNW buyers do not wait. | "Request a Private Viewing" or "Arrange a Private Introduction" |
| "Now For Sale" | Transactional. Mass-market. Indistinguishable from a billboard for a suburban housing development. | "Available by introduction" |
| "Price reduction" / "incentive" / "discount" | Destroys the brand permanently in a single sentence. Confirms every suspicion the buyer has about distressed inventory. Once a discount is offered, the original price loses all credibility — not just for this unit, but for every unit in the building, including those already sold. Existing owners will hear about it. Referrals will stop. | Offer value additions that preserve headline pricing: design packages, MO membership upgrades, furniture packages. The buyer receives a private arrangement. The price remains intact. |
| "Hurry" / "Don't miss out" / "Last chance" | UHNW buyers despise artificial urgency. They have seen every sales tactic. Pressure signals desperation — and desperation is the opposite of luxury. | "We are scheduling private viewings through Q3." This creates a natural time boundary without pressure. |
| "The best views" / "The best units" | If even one sold unit has a superior view or layout, this claim collapses. The buyer tours the building, sees a sold penthouse with a better terrace, and loses trust permanently. Superlatives are promises that can be disproven. | Describe each unit's specific, verifiable qualities. "South-facing terrace overlooking the Stadtpark" is a fact. "The best view in Vienna" is a claim that invites contradiction. |
| "Investment opportunity" / "rental yield" / "capital appreciation" | Frames the residence as a financial product. Invites comparison with REITs, funds, or commercial property — comparisons you will lose. Also signals that the developer is selling to investors because end-users are not buying. | "A residence for those who intend to live exceptionally." If investment returns are relevant, present them in a private financial summary — never in marketing materials. |
| Anything implying early buyers got a better deal | Poisons existing owner relationships. Kills referral potential. If an owner hears that their purchase is now positioned as "Phase 1 at lower pricing," they feel cheated. Referrals — the single most powerful sales channel in ultra-luxury — cease immediately. | "Early owners validated the vision. The final collection benefits from their confidence and from the now-operational hotel." |

## PART 2: THE STRATEGIC REFRAME — "THE FOUNDERS' COLLECTION"

### A. The Thesis

The 9 remaining residences were never "left behind." The strategic reframe requires a credible narrative for why these specific units are available now — and that narrative must be true enough to withstand scrutiny from buyers who will conduct due diligence, consult their advisors, and ask pointed questions.

Three narratives are available. Each is credible. They can be used individually or combined depending on the audience:

**Narrative 1 — Developer Hold-Back**
"Brisen Group retained a selection of residences on its own balance sheet. Following the successful opening of the Mandarin Oriental hotel and the establishment of the residential community, we have elected to release a curated portfolio to the market."

This narrative works because developer hold-backs are standard practice in luxury development. Buyers in this segment have seen it at One Hyde Park, Aman Tokyo, and Four Seasons Private Residences globally. It reframes the units from "unsold" to "unreleased." It also subtly communicates financial strength — the developer could afford to hold.

**Narrative 2 — Phased Release**
"The initial tranche was offered exclusively to a closed circle of qualified buyers through private introduction. This is the first time these residences have been made available to the broader market."

This narrative works for buyers who were not part of the initial sales process. It flatters them — they are now being granted access — while explaining the gap without inviting questions about why units remain. It also provides a natural opening for broker introductions: "Your client now has access to residences that were previously unavailable."

**Narrative 3 — Post-Completion Premium**
"These residences were deliberately held until the building was complete and the hotel fully operational. Buyers acquire a proven asset — no construction risk, no renderings, no speculation. You walk through your future home today, exactly as it will be."

This is the strongest narrative for risk-averse UHNW buyers, particularly those who have been burned by off-plan purchases elsewhere. It turns what could be perceived as a weakness (completion without full sell-out) into a decisive advantage (certainty). The hotel is open. The service is tangible. The community exists. There is nothing left to imagine.

**Denis should select the appropriate narrative based on the buyer profile.** For European old money: Narrative 1 (institutional, understated). For Middle Eastern or Asian UHNW: Narrative 2 (exclusivity, access). For American or pragmatic buyers: Narrative 3 (certainty, proof).

### B. Naming Convention

The collection needs a name. Unnamed inventory is commodity. Named inventory is curated.

**Primary recommendation: "The Founders' Collection"**

The word "Founders" is deliberate. It frames buyers of the final 9 not as latecomers but as the founding members of a permanent community. The first 16 were early believers. The Founders' Collection buyers are the ones who complete the vision — the community could not exist without them.

Alternative options if "Founders" does not resonate:
- **"The Private Collection"** — clean, understated, works well for European audiences
- **"The Courthouse Collection"** — ties directly to the heritage building, distinctive, memorable
- **"The Belvedere Collection"** — if the units have views toward the Belvedere Palace, this creates an immediate mental image

The chosen name must appear consistently across: website, brochures, email signatures from the sales team, broker packages, and any press materials. It is not a tagline. It is the proper noun by which these residences are known.

### C. Name Every Residence

This is non-negotiable. Stop selling "Unit 4B." A unit number is an address. A name is a home.

Each of the 9 residences receives:

**1. A proper name** drawn from the building's heritage, its position, or its defining architectural feature. Examples:

- "The Belvedere Penthouse" — if it faces the Belvedere gardens
- "The Ringstrasse Suite" — if it overlooks the Ring
- "The Courthouse Garden Residence" — if it has access to the interior courtyard
- "The Stadtpark Terrace" — if its terrace faces the park
- "The Salon Residence" — if it features the original courtroom restored as a living space
- "The Gallery" — if it has an elongated layout suited to art display
- "The Loggia" — if it features a distinctive covered terrace or balcony

The names must be specific, evocative, and verifiable. A buyer who visits "The Belvedere Penthouse" must be able to see the Belvedere. Names that overpromise will be exposed in the first viewing.

**2. A hero image** — real photography of the actual residence, not renderings. If the unit is unfurnished, commission styled photography (see Part 6). An unfurnished photograph is honest but uninspiring. A rendered image is inspiring but dishonest at this stage. Styled photography is both honest and inspiring.

**3. A one-paragraph architectural story** — what makes this specific residence extraordinary. Not marketing copy. A genuine description of the space, its light, its proportions, its relationship to the building and the city. Written by someone who has stood in the room. Example:

*"The Stadtpark Terrace occupies the southwest corner of the fourth floor, where two walls of original floor-to-ceiling windows frame an uninterrupted view across the treetops of the Stadtpark to the spire of the Karlskirche beyond. The 42-square-metre wrap-around terrace — the largest in the building — catches afternoon sun from April through October. Inside, the restored parquet floors and 3.6-metre ceilings create a sense of volume that no new-build can replicate."*

**4. A downloadable floor plan** — available behind an email capture form. The floor plan is the most-requested document in luxury residential sales. Gating it behind email capture converts anonymous website visitors into qualified leads. The form should be minimal: name, email, phone (optional), "How did you hear about us?" dropdown.

**5. No price on the website.** Price on request only. This is standard practice in ultra-luxury. Displaying prices invites comparison shopping. Withholding them creates a conversation — and a conversation is the first step toward a viewing. The response to a price enquiry should be: "I would be happy to share the pricing and floor plans for [The Belvedere Penthouse]. May I arrange a brief call or a private viewing?"

## PART 5: PRICING STRATEGY

**Governing principle: Price at or above Phase 1 levels. Never discount.**

This is not stubbornness. It is brand mathematics.

If The Founders' Collection is priced below Phase 1, three things happen simultaneously:

1. The market concludes the developer is distressed. Every broker, every buyer, every journalist will interpret a price reduction as confirmation that the residences are unsold because they are overpriced. The "leftovers" narrative becomes fact.

2. Existing owners discover the reduction. In a community of 16 families, information travels. An owner who paid EUR 3.5M for their residence and discovers the neighbouring unit sold for EUR 3.0M will not refer their friends. They will consult their lawyer.

3. The reduction becomes the floor, not the ceiling. Once a discount is offered, every subsequent buyer negotiates from the discounted price downward. The original pricing is dead.

**Instead, offer value additions that preserve headline pricing:**

| Value Addition | Cost to Developer | Perceived Value to Buyer | Strategic Benefit |
|---------------|------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Complimentary interior design consultation with a named designer (e.g., Conran and Partners, Liaigre studio) | EUR 50,000-80,000 | EUR 150,000+ | Solves buyer's biggest post-purchase anxiety ("I have a EUR 4M shell — now what?") |
| 2 years complimentary MO Residence Club membership | EUR 10,000-15,000 | EUR 30,000-50,000 | Locks buyer into the MO ecosystem; increases satisfaction and referrals |
| Bespoke finishing package within an elevated material palette | EUR 30,000-60,000 | EUR 80,000-120,000 | Differentiates from Phase 1 — these units have "enhanced" specifications |
| Priority access to hotel services (private dining room, dedicated concierge, spa priority) | EUR 5,000-10,000/year | Priceless (status) | Creates emotional lock-in; buyer feels like a VIP, not a customer |
| Furniture package by a named design house (Minotti, Poliform, B&B Italia) | EUR 80,000-150,000 | EUR 120,000-200,000 | Buyer moves in immediately; eliminates 6-12 month furnishing process |
| Complimentary parking space (if available) | EUR 0 (existing inventory) | EUR 50,000-80,000 | High perceived value, zero marginal cost |

The buyer feels they received a private, bespoke arrangement. The headline price remains intact. The developer's margin is reduced modestly, but the brand — and the pricing integrity for the entire building — is preserved.

**For the penthouses and highest-value units:** Consider offering a "White Glove Package" that bundles design consultation, furniture, and a 6-month post-move concierge service. Position it as: "We do not simply sell you a residence. We deliver a home." This justifies the premium pricing while making the purchase feel effortless rather than expensive.

## APPENDIX A: COMPETITIVE PRECEDENTS

The following case studies demonstrate that the challenge MORV faces is neither unusual nor insurmountable. Every major branded residence project has navigated the transition from "selling off-plan" to "selling a completed product." The ones that did it well reframed the narrative. The ones that did it poorly discounted.

| Developer / Project | Strategy | Outcome |
|--------------------|----------|---------|
| **One Hyde Park, London** | Held back the top-floor units for years after building completion. Released them as a "final phase" at prices significantly above Phase 1. Used scarcity as the exclusive message — no discounts, no urgency, no explanation for the delay. | Press covered the release as evidence of scarcity and desirability, not as evidence of slow sales. The final units sold at record per-square-foot prices. The "hold-back" narrative was accepted without question because the building's reputation had been established. |
| **Aman Tokyo Residences** | Released remaining units as a "second phase" — psychologically positioning them as a new launch rather than leftover inventory. Updated pricing to reflect the operational hotel premium. Emphasized the ability to experience the Aman lifestyle before committing. | Avoided any "unsold" narrative. Buyers perceived the second phase as an upgrade — they could see the product before buying. Press covered it as a new offering, not a continuation of an old one. |
| **Bulgari Residences, London and Dubai** | After completion, shifted all marketing from real estate language to lifestyle editorial. Stopped talking about square metres and floor plans. Started talking about the Bulgari Bar, the spa, the private cinema, the residents' garden. Residences became "membership" — not "units." | Sales accelerated post-completion because the marketing matched the buyer's emotional decision process. Buyers were not purchasing real estate; they were joining a club. The shift in language changed the buyer's frame from rational (price per sqm) to emotional (lifestyle identity). |
| **Four Seasons Private Residences, New Orleans** | Listed remaining units on MLS (the public real estate listing service). Opened inventory to general brokerage. Allowed brokers to market units alongside standard residential listings. | Brand premium destroyed. Once a Four Seasons residence appears on MLS next to a renovated townhouse, the luxury positioning collapses. Buyers negotiated aggressively, citing comparable listings. A cautionary tale for every branded residence developer. |
| **The Residences at Mandarin Oriental, Bangkok** | Post-completion, focused marketing on the hotel's restaurant (which earned a Michelin star) and the concierge service. Created a "Residence Experience" programme allowing prospective buyers to stay in the hotel and tour a furnished model unit. | The restaurant and service became the lead story. The residences were positioned as "the ultimate expression" of a lifestyle the buyer had already experienced. This eliminated the imagination gap — the buyer knew exactly what they were buying because they had lived it for two nights. |

**The lesson from every case:** After completion, stop selling real estate. Start selling a life. The building is no longer a rendering or a promise. It is a proven, tangible, liveable reality. That is the most powerful sales tool available — but only if the language, the website, the collateral, and the sales team are all aligned to present it as such.
